What is a Cyclone?

A cyclone is a large-scale air mass that rotates around a strong center of low pressure. Cyclones can cause widespread destruction, particularly through strong winds, heavy rainfall, and storm surges. They are categorized into tropical cyclones (formed over warm ocean waters) and extratropical cyclones (formed in cooler climates). Cyclones can bring dangerous winds, flooding, and power outages, and are often accompanied by tornadoes and heavy storms.

Types of Cyclones:

  • Tropical Cyclones: These cyclones form in tropical and subtropical areas, with strong winds and heavy rainfall.
  • Extratropical Cyclones: These cyclones develop in higher latitudes and can cause severe weather like snowstorms and blizzards.
  • Tornadoes: Often associated with cyclones, these small-scale but very destructive storms are characterized by rotating columns of air.

Impact of Cyclones:

  • Widespread flooding due to heavy rainfall and storm surges.
  • Destruction of infrastructure and buildings due to strong winds.
  • Power outages and communication disruptions.
  • Displacement of communities and loss of life.
  • Environmental damage such as soil erosion and loss of wildlife habitats.

Before a Cyclone

  • Stay informed by following weather forecasts and cyclone warnings.
  • Prepare an emergency kit with food, water, medications, and other essentials.
  • Ensure your home is secure by reinforcing windows and doors.
  • Evacuate early if authorities issue an evacuation order.
  • Have a family emergency plan and know multiple evacuation routes.

During a Cyclone

  • Stay indoors in a safe, strong, and sheltered area.
  • Avoid windows and glass doors, and stay away from the storm's path.
  • If you are caught outside, seek immediate shelter in a building or vehicle.
  • Listen to official broadcasts and instructions from authorities.
  • If flooding occurs, move to higher ground and avoid floodwaters.

After a Cyclone

  • Wait for official confirmation that it is safe to leave your shelter.
  • Check for hazards such as downed power lines or unstable buildings.
  • Help others in need, particularly the elderly or those with disabilities.
  • Follow local authorities' guidance on when and how to return home.
  • Report damages to emergency services and assist in cleanup efforts.
